A customer brought her violin to an old master for repair.

The master's favorite student was in the shop. She was a once-in-a-century mind, with a near-photographic memory across crafts and an endless supply of ideas. What she had learned in shipbuilding, clockmaking, joinery, restoration, and other trades stayed ready in her mind, and she could connect techniques no ordinary expert would think to join.

When she saw the violin, she said, "I can make this playable by nightfall."

The master said, "How?"

The apprentice said, "In the shipyards they save a cracked beam by fastening new wood to it and carrying the strain elsewhere. I would build a light inner frame from end to end, bind the broken parts to it, reset the neck against that frame, and let the old body ride upon the new. It would be stronger than before."

The master took the violin and looked at it for a long time.

Then he said, "It would work."

The apprentice smiled.

The master said, "It would work, but we will not use that method."

He gave the violin back and said, "You have solved the problem of making a playable instrument. The real problem is to repair this violin."

An LLM can be understood from first principles as a learned prior plus current context. The prior is the web of patterns it has absorbed from text: explanations, code, arguments, procedures, styles, mistakes, repairs, and examples of many kinds. The context is the prompt and surrounding material that define the situation at hand. From those two things it produces plausible next steps. Because the prior contains patterns from many distant domains, the model can sometimes bring structure from elsewhere and offer a striking answer that no specialist in the immediate problem had considered.

That same mechanism explains why an answer can be brilliant and still wrong for the real task. The model continues from the objective represented in the words it has. If that objective is only a proxy for what matters, the answer may satisfy the proxy while missing the actual need. If the request is framed as making something playable, it may find an ingenious way to make it playable. If the real aim is to repair this violin, preserving identity rather than mere function, then the answer misses the mark. Users still provide what plausibility cannot: the framing of the problem, the meaning of success, the taste to choose among good answers, the verification that checks whether an answer works, and the responsibility for what is done with it.
